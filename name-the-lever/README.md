# Name the lever

An interactive e-learning activity for **ABC Training & Consulting — Course 1.9 · Lesson 2: Phishing & Social Engineering** (prepared for ALS).

## What it teaches

Phishing and social-engineering messages work by pulling a psychological **lever** —
Authority, Urgency, Fear, Curiosity, Helpfulness or Familiarity. This activity shows six
realistic message snippets one at a time and asks the learner to **name** the lever each
one is pulling. The core takeaway: naming the pressure is what makes it resistible — an
unnamed feeling of urgency just makes you comply faster, but once you can say "that's
authority" or "that's familiarity", the pull loses its grip and you can slow down and
check.

The tone is deliberately warm: nobody is stupid for being fooled. Each snippet is written
to sound like something a reasonable person *would* have acted on.

## Mechanic

**Classify** · 6 items · ~4 minutes. Read the snippet, click the lever you think it is
pulling. You commit before any explanation appears; the chosen button turns green
(correct) or red (wrong) and the correct answer is always highlighted with a ✓, followed
by a short "why" panel. The final item adds an extra teaching beat about where attackers
get their "familiar" details, then a closing line. A **Start again** button replays it.

## How to open

Just open **`index.html`** by double-clicking it in any modern browser. There is no build
step, no dependencies and no network connection required — everything (HTML, CSS, JS) is
inline in the single file, and it runs fully offline from `file://`.

## Notes

- All senders, domains, people, instruments and results are **fictional**.
- Before publishing, ALS should localise any reporting contacts / incident-response steps
  referenced in the wider lesson so learners know exactly who to tell when they spot one
  of these messages in real life.
